01

Why value disappears
after signature

The problem
It's not one thing. It's seven overlapping failures that happen quietly, every day, after a contract is signed. The current product doesn't surface any of them.
Hidden work
Obligations buried in PDFs
Every contract has 40–80 obligations hidden in the middle of an 80-page PDF. Nobody re-reads after signing. They show up only when something breaks.
Hard to find
The right clause is unfindable
Even with search, the clause you need is buried under defined terms and cross-references. Lawyers learn to hunt by keyword — everyone else gives up.
Missed deadlines
Renewals happen without warning
The vendor auto-renews at 12% higher. Procurement finds out when the invoice arrives. By then, the new price is legally locked in.
"We've lost six figures three years running on a single SaaS auto-renew nobody set a reminder for." — Head of Procurement
Too many tools
The contract lives in five systems
SAP for payments. Salesforce for the customer. JIRA for delivery. ServiceNow for SLA tickets. DocuSign for the PDF. No single place to see the full picture.
No early warning
Problems spotted too late
Vendor emails get tense. Invoices arrive late. Delivery slips quietly. These are signals a dispute is coming — and nobody is watching for them.
Audit scramble
Compliance is always last-minute
Every audit triggers a 3-week scramble to gather security certs, verify data processors, and prove compliance. It's never tracked continuously. Always reactive.
No connected view
One deal, six folders
A single deal might have: a master agreement, five statements of work, four amendments, a data agreement, an NDA, and seven change orders — spread across six folders. No system shows it as one continuous relationship with a single health signal.

Five types of people.
Five different needs.
One broken start.

The insight
Every team that uses Sirion post-signature is looking for something different. A contract manager needs to track obligations. Legal needs to monitor risk. Procurement needs to catch renewals. Finance needs to match invoices. IT/Security needs to track data compliance. But right now, everyone starts from the same screen — a list of documents — and digs from there. That's the foundational problem.

The design problem in one sentence
Each person should open Sirion and immediately see their thing — not a list of documents to dig through.
The home screen should reflect how each person actually works. But everything still ties back to the same shared contracts underneath. Start from your role. Land on the contract. Never lose the thread.

How we chose
We explored five different ways to restructure the post-signature experience. Each was evaluated on how well it serves all five personas, how it handles the document as the legal record, how it works in everyday use, and how it supports AI assistance without replacing human judgement.

Task-first
Every contract becomes a list of tasks. The home screen is a work queue. Switching between personas changes which tasks you see.

Persona workspaces

Timeline-first
Time is the main axis. Every persona sees a timeline of events from their contracts. Looking back shows history. Looking forward shows what's coming.

Similar toOutlook, Cron, Icertis

E
AI briefing
Every morning, the AI generates a one-page summary for each persona: what's due, what's at risk, what needs a decision. The contract list is just a drill-down — the briefing is the home.
Home screenA daily AI-written summary
Similar toStripe Atlas, Notion AI
25 / 36
Executives love it. Power users and legal teams find it frustrating — they need to see the actual data, not a summary they can't verify.
Where AI fits instead
Making AI the only way in doesn't work for legal teams who need to point to specific clauses and data. But AI as a layer inside each workspace — a daily briefing at the top of your workspace — does work. The structure is always visible and verifiable. The AI just helps you focus on what matters today.
04

The direction.
Five workspaces.
One contract underneath.

What we're building
Five workspaces built for each team — Obligations, Risk, Renewals, Finance, Data. Each one shows a different view of the same contracts. Every item in every workspace links back to the contract clause that created it. The contract is always one click away. AI adds a daily briefing inside each workspace, but the full structure is always there to inspect.
What success looks like
"Open your workspace. See what matters today. Click through to the contract. Act."
Contract Manager
Opens obligations workspace → sees everything due this week → clicks to the clause that created it → sees the owner → closes it out. One flow, no folders.
Procurement
Opens renewals workspace → sees every agreement expiring in the next 90 days → sorted by financial risk → clicks through to the renewal window → takes action before it closes. Never surprised again.
IT / Security
Opens data workspace → sees every third-party data processor on a live contract → clicks to the data agreement clause → triggers a cert refresh. Continuous, not reactive.
01
Start from your role.
Always land on the contract.
Your workspace opens on what you care about — obligations, renewals, risk, whatever. But every item links back to the contract clause that governs it. You never lose the thread between your work and the legal record.
02
Structure first.
AI on top.
AI can only reason about what the system already knows. We build the relationship structure first — which documents govern which, which amendments apply where, what's active and what's superseded. Then AI uses that structure to surface what matters. Not the other way around.
03
The document is the record.
Never change it.
The contract PDF is the legal source of truth. Notes go in the margin. Tracked changes are separate. The system wraps it with context and intelligence — but the original document is always there, untouched. Lawyers can always point to a clause and a line number.
04
One deal.
One health picture.
A master agreement, five statements of work, four amendments, and seven change orders — that's one deal. The system shows it as one relationship with one health score. You see the whole picture without opening a single folder.
05
Warn early.
Don't report late.
The platform watches for early signals — a vendor's tone in emails, a late invoice, a milestone slipping, a renewal window opening. These are flagged before they become problems. The goal is to make invisible things visible before they cost money.
05

What we still
need to figure out.

Next decisions
Choosing the persona workspace approach settles the big structure. But it opens six specific questions that need answers before design can start in earnest.
01
Moving between workspaces — How does someone switch from the renewals workspace to the risk workspace without losing where they were? Sidebar navigation? A global search shortcut? A breadcrumb that shows the path back?
02
The shared contract page — When five workspaces all link to the same contract, what does that contract page look like? Does it stay the same for everyone, or does it adapt to show what's relevant to the person opening it?
03
Where the AI briefing lives — Inside each workspace, where exactly does the daily AI summary go? At the top of the page? In a side panel? Only on login? Does it stay or disappear after you've seen it?
04
People who wear multiple hats — A VP of Legal who also handles renewals. A contract manager who covers finance. Do they get a combined view, or toggle between separate workspaces? How do we avoid confusion when contexts overlap?
05
Defining "health" — A single health score for each deal sounds good. But what goes into it? Obligation completion rate, renewal risk, SLA status, open disputes? Who decides what matters most, and can teams configure it?
06
The other tools in the picture — SAP, Salesforce, JIRA, ServiceNow, and DocuSign are all live in these teams. Do we pull their data into Sirion, or link out? Which data does Sirion own, and which does it just reference?
Possible directions — one hint per question
#
The question
A possible direction
01
Moving between workspaces without losing context
A persistent contract anchor — a thin strip that travels with you across workspaces, always showing which contract you're currently inside. Switching workspace changes the lens; the contract stays pinned.
02
The shared contract page — same for everyone or persona-adaptive?
Fixed structure, contextual surface. The document, hierarchy, and amendments are always the same. The Today tab pre-filters signals by who's logged in — Legal sees risk, Procurement sees renewals — without changing the underlying page.
03
Where the AI briefing lives inside each workspace
Dismiss-and-remember. Appears at the top on first login each day, collapses after you interact with it, and is gone until tomorrow. Always recallable from a small chip in the nav. Never blocks the workspace.
04
People who wear multiple hats
Role stacking, not role switching. A "My work" mode that surfaces obligations from all their active roles in one prioritised list. Workspaces still exist for going deep — but the daily surface is unified.
05
Defining a single health score
Sensible default, transparent breakdown. Default weights: obligations on time (40%) · SLA compliance (30%) · no active disputes (20%) · renewal risk (10%). Always shown with components — never just a number. Teams can adjust weights.
06
The other tools — pull data in or link out?
Reference, don't own. Sirion shows status signals from SAP, JIRA, Salesforce as linked context — not a replica. Sirion owns the contract relationship graph. Other tools own their objects. Sirion surfaces what you need to know, not everything that exists.
06

The bets we're making —
and how we'll know we're right.

Design strategy
Every design direction is a bet. Being explicit about what we're betting on — and what would tell us we're wrong — is what separates a strategy from a preference. These are the commitments this direction rests on.
B·01
People work from their role,
not from a list of contracts.
We're betting that a Contract Manager opening Sirion wants to see obligations first — not a contract list they then filter. If this is wrong, we'll see it when users bypass the workspace home and navigate directly to search or contract pages instead. That's the signal to reconsider.
B·02
Structure enables AI.
AI on an unstructured system creates noise.
We're betting that AI summaries are only trusted when users can verify the underlying data. If users start dismissing the briefing without reading it, or escalating AI-generated signals as incorrect, we haven't built enough structure for the AI to reason over reliably.
B·03
Five workspaces stay coherent
because the contract page holds them together.
We're betting that a unified contract page — reachable from every workspace — prevents fragmentation. If users report confusion about which workspace "owns" a contract, or if the same contract appears differently in two workspaces without explanation, that's the seam breaking. The contract page must be the single source of truth.
B·04
Proactive signals reduce leakage
better than better search.
We're betting that surfacing what matters before users ask is more valuable than helping them find things faster. We already know better search didn't move the needle. If proactive signals also fail to drive action — if people see the warning and still miss the SLA window — the problem is accountability, not interface design. That's a different fix.
B·05
Reference beats ownership
when it comes to other tools.
We're betting that showing "Invoice matched in SAP ✓" is more useful than pulling invoice data into Sirion. If users consistently need to leave Sirion to take action in the other tool — and can't do so in one click — the reference model is too shallow and we need deeper integration for specific high-frequency actions.
What good looks like — 6 months in
The signals that tell us
the direction is working.
Signal 01
Users open their workspace home first — not search or contract list — as their primary daily entry point.
Signal 02
SLA credit claims are filed within the window. Renewal decisions happen before notice dates. The system warns early enough that people act.
Signal 03
AI briefing read-rate stays above 60% after 30 days. Users who dismiss it do so because it's not relevant today — not because they don't trust it.
Signal 04
Users cross-link between workspaces naturally — a procurement user following an obligation chain into Legal's workspace — without getting lost.
Signal 05
The contract page is the most-visited page across all workspaces. It means the structure is holding — everyone is grounding their work in the same source of truth.
The question
worth asking last
If a Contract Manager opens Sirion tomorrow and sees their obligations — not a folder, not a list of PDFs, but the three things that need action today and the clause behind each one — does it feel like a better world? That's the bar.
